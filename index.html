It was supposed to be unbreakable.

02  ·  Why encryption

Some words are not meant to be read.

Not by everyone, anyway.

People have hidden their writing for as long as they have written. A merchant guarding a price. A general guarding a plan. A lover guarding a name.

VDKI CS NB HYU GAZJPL

The goal never changes: make the words unreadable to everyone but the one person who should read them. That trick is a cipher, and the oldest one still has Caesar's name on it.

A bigger lock.

05  ·  The break

But frequencies don't lie.

A substitution scrambles which letter is which — but not how often each appears. In English, E is far and away the most common letter, then T, then A. Those peaks survive the scramble; they just wear a different mask. Count the ciphertext and your suspects line themselves up.

06  ·  Vigenère

A cipher that changes its mind.

What if the shift changed with every keystroke? Write a key word under the message, repeating it, and let each key letter set the shift for the letter above it (A = 0, B = 1, … Z = 25). Now a repeated plaintext letter usually encrypts to something different each time, and the shape of English dissolves.

07  ·  One rotor

A cipher you don't have to remember.

A rotor is a wheel: 26 contacts on each face, 26 wires inside scrambling one to the other. The wiring is fixed at the factory — but the wheel turns. Every keypress advances it one notch, so the same key now flows through a different wire and lights a different letter. Press one letter 26 times and you get 26 different outputs.

This is the real Rotor I wiring — EKMFLGDQVZNTOWYHXUSPAIBRCJ — used in 1939.

08  ·  Many rotors

Three wheels, all turning.

Stack three. The signal threads through all of them in turn. The fast rotor steps every keypress; the middle one only when the fast rotor completes a turn (~26 keys); the slow one only when the middle completes a turn (~676). Three odometer wheels at three speeds — the pattern won't repeat for 17,576 letters.

09  ·  Reflector

The signal bounces back.

Behind the rotors sits a fixed wiring that pairs letters and sends the current back the way it came — through all three rotors again, in reverse. Because the path is mirrored, the machine is a mirror: whatever it does to a letter it undoes if you repeat it. The same machine encrypts and decrypts.

10  ·  Plugboard

Patch cables for the alphabet.

Before a letter reaches the rotors it is swapped with another; after it returns it is swapped again. Operators ran about ten cables on a panel of 26 jacks. Each cable is just a fixed swap — the very first trick from five sections ago — but stacked on top of the rotors it multiplies the machine's settings enormously.

13  ·  And yet

It fell.

A hundred and fifty-eight quintillion settings, changed every midnight — and by mid-war Bletchley Park was reading German traffic in hours. Not by brute force. By shrinking the search.

The first wedge was the reflector itself. Because the current always bounces back, no letter can ever encrypt to itself. A is never A. T is never T. Not for any rotor setting, any plugboard, ever — a rule the machine cannot break.

01  ·  The crib

A crib is plaintext you can guess. German weather stations opened the 6 a.m. report with WETTERBERICHT. Bletchley didn't know where it sat in the ciphertext — but any alignment where a crib letter lands on the same ciphertext letter is impossible. Slide it and watch positions get ruled out for free:

02  ·  The bombe

For each surviving alignment, Turing's bombe — stacks of Enigma rotors spinning in parallel, each a guess — searched for a rotor setting consistent with the crib, and stopped when it found one. The cipher was strong; the system around it, with its predictable operators, was not.

03  ·  Afterwards

The intelligence, codenamed Ultra, is reckoned to have shortened the war by a year or more, and stayed secret for thirty. The machine above is mechanically real. The history is too. The arithmetic was bigger than the people who broke it. They broke it anyway — with patience, machinery, and one tiny rule.
